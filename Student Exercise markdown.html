Background Concepts** ## **4.1 What a Melting Curve Represents** A DNA melting curve shows how a population of DNA molecules changes from **fully double-stranded** to **fully single-stranded** as temperature increases. The simulator reports **Fraction Melted**, ranging from: - **0.0 ‚Üí fully dsDNA** - **1.0 ‚Üí fully ssDNA** Plotting Fraction Melted vs Temperature produces a **sigmoidal (S-shaped)** curve with three characteristic regions. --- ## **4.2 How Melting Curves Are Generated** Melting occurs in three major stages: ### **1. Low-Temperature Baseline** DNA remains fully double-stranded Fraction Melted ‚âà 0 ### **2. Cooperative Transition Region** Local regions begin to denature Melting in one region destabilizes nearby regions ‚Üí steep slope ### **3. High-Temperature Plateau** DNA is fully melted Fraction Melted approaches 1 Underlying calculations use the **SantaLucia nearest-neighbor model**, which sums enthalpy/entropy contributions of adjacent nucleotide pairs. --- ## **4.3 Melting Temperature (Tm)** **Tm is the temperature at which 50% of the population is melted.** - On the melting curve ‚Üí Fraction Melted ‚âà 0.5 - On the derivative curve ‚Üí corresponds to the **peak** Higher Tm ‚Üí more stable sequence Lower Tm ‚Üí less stable sequence --- ## **4.4 GC vs AT Melting Behavior** GC content has a strong effect on stability: - **GC base pairs (3 H-bonds)** ‚Üí higher stability - **AT base pairs (2 H-bonds)** ‚Üí lower stability Therefore: - GC-rich sequences melt at **higher temperatures** - AT-rich sequences melt at **lower temperatures** --- ## **4.5 Short vs Long DNA** ### **Short DNA (<100 bp)** - Melts in one cooperative step - Single, smooth sigmoidal curve - One derivative peak ### **Long DNA (>200 bp)** - Contains multiple regions with different GC content - AT-rich regions melt first - GC-rich regions melt later - Produces **multi-phase** melting Derivative curves may show **2 or more peaks**, each representing a melting domain. --- ## **4.6 Multiple Melting Domains** Longer DNA often melts in distinct phases: - early melting of AT-rich domains - later melting of GC-rich domains This results in: - multi-step melting curves - visible shoulders - multiple derivative peaks --- ## **4.7 First-Derivative Curves (‚ÄìdF/dT)** Derivative curves highlight: - where melting occurs most rapidly - approximate Tm - number of melting domains Short DNA ‚Üí single sharp peak Long DNA ‚Üí multiple peaks --- ## **4.8 Factors Influencing Melting** Several conditions shift melting curves: | Factor | Effect | |--------|--------| | ‚Üë Na‚Å∫ | stabilizes dsDNA ‚Üí ‚Üë Tm | | ‚Üë Mg¬≤‚Å∫ | strong stabilization ‚Üí ‚Üë‚Üë Tm | | DMSO / formamide | destabilize dsDNA ‚Üí ‚Üì Tm | | Mismatches | biologically lower Tm (but **not modeled** here) | | Repeats/homopolymers | tend to melt more uniformly | --- ## **4.9 High-Resolution Melting (HRM)** HRM can detect extremely small sequence differences (e.g., SNPs) by analyzing: - subtle shifts in Tm - changes in curve shape Even a **single nucleotide change** may alter the melting curve. --- # üñºÔ∏è **5. Glossary** ### **Tm (Melting Temperature)** Temperature at which 50% of DNA molecules are melted. ### **Fraction Melted** Proportion of molecules that are single-stranded at a given temperature. ### **dsDNA** Double-stranded DNA. ### **ssDNA** Single-stranded DNA. ### **Cooperative Melting** Melting in one region destabilizes adjacent regions. ### **Baseline** Low-temperature region where DNA is fully duplexed. ### **Plateau** High-temperature region where DNA is fully single-stranded. ### **Melting Domain** A region of a long DNA molecule that melts at a characteristic temperature. ### **First-Derivative Curve (‚ÄìdF/dT)** A curve showing the rate of melting; peaks correspond to melting transitions and Tm values. ---
